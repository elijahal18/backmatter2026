## When being offline becomes a social status for the rich, what does it mean for those without a choice?

The first personal computer felt like a marker of entry into a new world—forward, connected, and full of possibility. But by the early 2000s, we entered the “digital divide,” which asked a simple question: Who had access? Treated as an urgent social problem, policymakers and educators pushed to bring computers and internet access into schools, particularly for low-income and marginalized communities. The assumption was simple: more access means more opportunity. U.S. Census reports from the period showed that access was sharply unequal, with lower rates of computer ownership and home internet use among low-income households, raising concerns that existing inequalities would be reproduced in digital form. But eventually, everyone caught up. Today, the divide is no longer about who is online, but who is allowed offline. While the working class is managed by a boss in their pocket with an algorithm that interprets logging off as a detrimental form of absence, the upper echelons of society can pay thousands for digital detox retreats in search of “authenticity.” At places like Urban Cowboy Lodge in New York’s Catskills, guests can trade their phones for an instant camera, cassette player, and paper map. Or at Eremito in Umbria, Italy, a former monastery turned hotel with no Wi-Fi, no phone signal, and silent communal dinners.

In November 2025, Vogue Business published “How ‘Unplugging’ Became Luxury’s Most Valuable Currency,” tracing what it describes as a broader cultural turn away from constant digital engagement. In the era of “AI slop and algorithm fatigue,” writer Amy Francombe suggests going offline has become the latest “status symbol.” The shift is situated within a growing sense of digital fatigue—particularly among younger consumers—who are increasingly drawn to analog experiences as a way to counter the overstimulation of algorithm-driven feeds. Whether it’s phone-free parties or the resurgence of zines, flip phones, and other tactile forms of media, what is emerging is not a rejection of technology but a recalibration of how it is used.

Fashion, as Francombe suggests, has been quick to translate this mood into experience. Across 2024, a number of fashion shows and brand events introduced no-phone policies, asking guests to step into environments where documentation is limited and attention is redirected. One example is The Row’s Resort 2025 collection, which asked guests not to photograph; a move Vogue described as “the ultimate act of quiet luxury.” The brand repeated the gesture at Paris Fashion Week in 2025, once again limiting real-time documentation and turning attention back to the room itself. These spaces are designed to feel immersive—less mediated, more immediate—but they also reintroduce exclusivity. Access becomes physical, bounded, and time-specific. 

Similarly, Business Insider described a similar shift in social terms, arguing that “Logging Off Is the New Luxury,” as companies like 222, Timeleft, Kanso, and Sofar Sounds build curated in-person experiences around people’s desire to step away from apps—making reduced digital visibility read less like absence and more like a sign of intention, control, and even status.

Virginia Eubanks, a political scientist whose work focuses on technology, inequality, and the automated management of poverty, writes in her 2018 book, Automating Inequality, of the “digital poorhouse”—the space which includes many of the systems people rely on for basic survival—welfare, housing assistance, and child services. Eubanks draws a direct line between these contemporary systems and the historical poorhouse—institutions designed not just to provide relief, but to regulate and discipline the poor. Today, that structure hasn’t disappeared; it’s been digitized. 

For those navigating these systems, being offline isn’t freedom. Missing an email, failing to upload a document, or being unreachable can mean losing access to essential services altogether. Eubanks writes that Indiana’s automated welfare system denied roughly one million applications for health care, food stamps, and cash benefits in just three years, treating routine mistakes or missed steps as “failure to cooperate.” She recounts the case of Omega Young, a woman who missed a required phone recertification appointment while hospitalized with terminal cancer and, as a result, lost access to Medicaid and food stamps.

The same digital infrastructures that promise connection also enforce compliance. Digital platforms don’t just distribute resources; they monitor and evaluate the people using them. It doesn’t eliminate bias; it embeds it more deeply, as the system remembers, records, and follows.

Similar concerns are echoed within gig work. While initially appearing flexible on the surface, underneath it’s structured by constant, low-level pressure. Tasks are distributed through opaque systems, performance is measured through ratings and metrics, and workers are nudged toward behaviors that maximize efficiency for the platform. The ability to earn is contingent on remaining constantly available to an invisible authority. This is especially true  when logging off or deactivation means missing higher-paying requests, losing priority in the system, and falling behind in rankings that determine future access to work. There isn’t a manager to appeal to or an office to visit—just the system to decide.

Labor scholar Alex J. Wood uses the term “algorithmic control” to describe how gig platforms replace many of the traditional functions of a boss—supervision, discipline, and performance evaluation—with software systems. It refers to the delegation of managerial power to automated systems that track behavior, assign tasks, and evaluate performance in real time. Research on platform labor—particularly Michael Walker, Peter Fleming, and Marco Berti’s study of Uber drivers in the journal Organization—argues that algorithms govern workers not just as employees, but as bodies in motion by monitoring routes, response times, acceptance rates, and even patterns of rest. Platforms like Uber, DoorDash, and Instacart don’t simply connect workers to jobs; they manage them.

Over the past decade, several countries in Europe have introduced “right to disconnect” labor protections, meant to address the expectation that workers should always be reachable. In France, the right was incorporated into labor law in 2016 and requires companies to establish boundaries around after-hours communication. In Spain and Portugal similar protections were adopted in 2018 and 2022 as a response to an always-on digital work culture. The United States has no federal equivalent.

New York City attempted a “right to disconnect” proposal in 2018 that would have protected employees from after-hours electronic communications, but it never became law. Instead, the right still functions as a workplace perk, rather than a guarantee, available to white-collar workers with the leverage to set boundaries without risking their income.

That leaves a much broader group of workers in a familiar in-between. Traditional office workers without the salaries or autonomy of senior professionals are still expected to stay responsive after hours, even when their finances are tight and their time is not fully their own. Their boundaries are informal, fragile, and dependent on workplace culture rather than law. For gig workers, freelancers, and independent contractors, the situation is starker. Their labor is governed by classifications that treat flexibility as a feature, even when it functions as a requirement. So the question is not simply, Who can log off? It is, Who can do so without consequence? The answer still follows the lines that have long structured labor: class, stability, and power.
