title: Who Gets to Log Off?
author: N/A
---

The first computer felt like a marker of entry into a new world—forward, connected, and full of possibility. But by the early 2000s, what entered the “digital divide” which asked a simple question: Who had access? Treated as an urgent social problem, policymakers and educators pushed to bring computers and internet access into schools, particularly for low-income and marginalized communities.  The assumption was simple: more access means more opportunity. Articles in Wired and reports from the U.S. Census tracked whether the “have-nots” would ever catch up. And eventually, many did.

Today, the divide is no longer about who is online, but who is allowed to be offline. While the upper echelons of society pay thousands for digital detox retreats in search of “authenticity,” the working class is managed by a boss in their pocket and an algorithm that interprets logging off as a form of absence, even refusal.

In late 2025, Vogue Business published “How ‘Unplugging’ Became Luxury’s Most Valuable Currency,” tracing what it describes as a broader cultural turn away from constant digital engagement. The article situates this shift within a growing sense of digital fatigue—particularly among younger consumers—who are increasingly drawn to analog experiences as a way to counter the overstimulation of algorithm-driven feeds.  From phone-free parties to the resurgence of zines, flip phones, and other tactile forms of media, what is emerging is less a rejection of technology than a recalibration of how it is used.

Fashion, as the article suggests, has been quick to translate this mood into experience. Across 2024, a number of shows and brand events introduced no-phone policies, asking guests to step into environments where documentation is limited and attention is redirected. These spaces are designed to feel immersive—less mediated, more immediate—but they also quietly reintroduce exclusivity as access becomes physical, bounded, and time-specific. Similarly, Business Insider has reported this shift in social terms, suggesting that a reduced digital presence can signal the ability to exist and even succeed without constant online performance. Making disconnection a way to assert control over visibility, not absence..

But this assumes that stepping away from the internet is actually an option.

Political scientist Virginia Eubanks argues in her work, the “digital poorhouse,” that many of the systems people rely on for basic survival—welfare, housing assistance, child services, etc.—are now mediated through digital platforms that don’t just distribute resources, but monitor and evaluate the people using them. 

Eubanks draws a direct line between these contemporary systems and the historical poorhouse—institutions designed not just to provide relief, but to regulate and discipline the poor. Today, that structure hasn’t disappeared; it’s been digitized. Automated systems sort, score, and flag individuals, often determining who is eligible for support and who is not, sometimes with life-altering consequences.  The promise of neutrality within data and algorithms doesn’t eliminate bias,it embeds it more deeply into the infrastructure itself.

For those navigating these systems, being offline isn’t freedom. Missing an email, failing to upload a document, or being unreachable can mean losing access to essential services altogether. The same infrastructures that promise connection also enforce compliance. Unlike the curated “detox” imagined by celebrity retreats or at high-class fashion shows, there’s no clean break here. The system remembers. It records. It follows.

What scholars describe as “algorithmic management” refers to the delegation of managerial power to automated systems that track behavior, assign tasks, and evaluate performance in real time.  This is similar to  Michel Foucault’s concept of biopower—a form of power that operates not through force, but through the regulation of everyday life, shaping how people move, act, and organize their time. Recent research on platform labor argues that algorithms function in precisely this way, governing workers not just as employees, but as bodies in motion by monitoring routes, response times, acceptance rates, and even patterns of rest. Platforms like Uber, DoorDash, and Instacart don’t simply connect workers to jobs; they manage them. 

While gigwork appears flexible on the surface, underneath it’s structured by constant, low-level pressure. Tasks are distributed through opaque systems, performance is measured through ratings and metrics, and workers are nudged toward behaviors that maximize efficiency for the platform. The ability to earn is contingent on remaining constantly available to an invisible authority. Especially when logging off or deactivation  means missing higher-paying requests, losing priority in the system, and falling behind in rankings that determine future access to work. There isn’t a manager to appeal to or an office to visit–just the system to decide.

Over the past decade, several countries including Spain, Portugal, and most notably France have introduced the “Right to Disconnect”: a labor protection that allows employees to ignore work-related communications outside of working hours without facing penalties. In France, this right was formally incorporated into the labor code in 2016, requiring companies to establish boundaries around after-hours communication and affirming that workers cannot be disciplined for being unreachable outside their designated time. In Spain and Portugal, the right to disconnect is framed as a necessary response to an “always-on” digital work culture.  But its protections are unevenly applied. 

Initially the right to disconnect reads as an update of older labor rights for the modern digital age, but these laws largely cover salaried employees within formal employment structures—people whose labor is already recognized and regulated. 

By contrast, the United States has no federal equivalent. While some proposals have surfaced at the state level, the “right to disconnect” remains more of a workplace perk than a legal guarantee reserved for white-collar workers who have the leverage to set boundaries without risking their income.  For gig workers, freelancers, and independent contractors, the situation is fundamentally different. The legal framework that governs their labor—shaped by classifications like “independent contractor”—positions flexibility as a feature, even when it functions as a requirement. There are no mandated off-hours, no protections against after-hours demands, and no clear recourse when constant availability becomes the expectation. 

Even the infrastructures that make disconnection possible are sustained by forms of labor that remain largely invisible, extending far beyond the gig worker to global supply chains—most notably in places like the Democratic Republic of the Congo, which produces the majority of the world’s cobalt, a necessary key component for the batteries in smartphones.

While higher-status workers are more likely to assert their right to disconnect without consequence, lower-wage workers face quieter forms of retaliation, receiving fewer shifts, fewer opportunities, and less visibility if they attempt to do the same.

The question isn’t, Who can log off? It’s, Who can do so without consequence? Theanswer follows the same lines that have always structured labor: class, stability, and power.
