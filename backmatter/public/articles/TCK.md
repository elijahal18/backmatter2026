---
title: Third Culture Kids
author: Katya Wack
---

When I was three, my family left Bethesda, Maryland and moved to Moscow, Russia. I was too young to understand politics, but old enough to absorb the atmosphere of a country in transition. For eight years, I lived in between cultures: American at home, Russian everywhere else. My name, Katya, fit into the roll call at school like any native’s. I recited Pushkin in the school yard, and carried flowers to my teachers on the Day of Knowledge. But beneath the ordinary rhythms of daily life, I felt a quiet dissonance: being both fully integrated and somehow separate.

I was not the only one navigating this in-between existence. Scholars refer to people like me as Third Culture Kids (TCKs): children raised for substantial parts of their childhood in a culture different from their parents’ country of origin. The number of TCKs has grown sharply in the past two decades, driven by geopolitical upheaval, new migration flows, and increasingly globalized workforces. Research on TCKs  has long highlighted a recurring emotional dynamic: adaptability and cultural fluency often come at the cost of a stable sense of belonging. 

As international mobility increases and geopolitical pressures intensify, more children will grow up negotiating these layered spaces. My years in Moscow don’t provide a universal framework, but they offer one window into how early experiences of cultural dissonance can shape a person’s sense of safety, identity, and place. The value in examining these small, routine moments is not to draw sweeping lessons, but in understanding what they reveal: that for many third culture kids, the world is learned twice, and the gaps between those lessons often matter more than the lessons themselves. 

My upbringing was shaped by a city in flux, still absorbing the shock of the Soviet Union’s collapse a decade earlier: its streets marked by both sudden wealth and lingering instability. Early 2000s Moscow felt like an uneasy overlay of eras: Soviet-era concrete apartment blocks jostling beneath neon billboards advertising mobile phones; foreign supermarkets full of imported cereal just steps from open-air markets where babushkas sold honey and pickles; Metro stations gilded with chandeliers and marble, leading to streets where graffiti and crumbling facades spoke of a different reality. On one block, malls boasted Western brands; on another, worn playgrounds bore witness to childhoods buried beneath economic turmoil. This was the Moscow my classmates called home and the Moscow I learned to navigate — fluently, but never quite naturally. 

I grew up constantly recalibrating. At school, I fit in: I spoke Russian without an accent, joined in games, shared jokes. But sometimes my lunchbox, filled with Betty Crocker brownies and peanut butter sandwiches, betrayed me. Once, at the schoolyard, a parent approached my mother at pickup and asked me, “Где твоя мама?” (“Where is your mother?”), gesturing at a woman walking another child home. When I pointed to my mother — her blonde hair and soft hesitation — the parent corrected gently: “Нет, это твоя няня.” (“No, that’s your nanny.”) I had to insist: “Это моя мама.” I was not conflicted; I was just different. 

At home it felt different. My parents spoke English among themselves; dinner conversation was sometimes interrupted by mispronounced Russian or hesitation. I would correct them — sometimes only in my head — not quite for them but for myself. The Russian phrases I brought home sometimes felt awkward, foreign, even wrong in the context of our small U.S.-rooted apartment. 

One early memory stands out: my first day of school, September 1st. I stood in line, ribbons in my hair, clutching a bouquet nearly as tall as I was, ready for the Day of Knowledge — the annual celebration that marked the beginning of the school year. Parents snapped photos. Teachers gave speeches. But midway, the mood changed. Whispered voices, stiffened faces. We were led out, evacuated to a nearby playground. Confusion rippled among children; bouquets were abandoned. Night brought television news: a massacre — a siege at a school in Beslan, hundreds of children held hostage, more than 330 dead. The deadliest school shooting in history, carried out by Chechen rebels. I was too young to grasp the full horror, but I felt something shift. 

Looking back, I recognize that much of the emotional weight I carried — the unease, the yearning to belong — is not unique to me. A 2022 review of research on TCKs shows that children raised internationally often struggle with belonging, social adjustment, and identity. Many describe feeling forever in between cultures, never fully accepted in any one. “Home” becomes less a place and more a question: “Where am I from?” “Where do I belong?”  But those questions also point to something more fundamental. If identity is formed across multiple cultures, belonging becomes harder to locate in any single one. It raises the possibility that belonging is not simply inherited through place or culture, but something constructed over time—assembled through movement, adaptation, and the shifting contexts in which a life unfolds.

Yet this outsider existence also shapes a particular kind of observer — someone finely attuned to nuance, contradiction, and the unstable layers beneath appearances. TCKs often develop sensitivity to context, an ability to code-switch, and a global perspective that can be an asset. 

In 2025, as mobility — economic, political, academic — increases globally, the TCK experience feels more relevant than ever. More families are crossing borders; more children grow up in cultures not their own. The tensions I felt as a child in Moscow — longing for belonging, balancing multiple identities, absorbing cultural contradictions — are now playing out in new forms all over the world. 

At the same time, the world has changed. Social media and virtual connection offer new forms of belonging: digital communities of fellow TCKs, shared stories across continents, support networks that transcend geography. A 2023 study found that many former TCKs draw on their multicultural identity to navigate college and adulthood successfully — their adaptability serving them well. What once felt like fragmentation can, over time, become a kind of resource—a way of moving through the world with flexibility, attentiveness, and an ability to hold multiple perspectives at once. If the experience of growing up between cultures begins in dissonance, it does not necessarily end there. Instead, it can lead to a reconfigured understanding of belonging—not as something fixed or inherited, but as something actively made. In that sense, the TCK experience gestures toward a form of rebirth: an identity not rooted in a single place, but shaped through transformation, through movement, and through the ongoing work of assembling a self across worlds.

But the core challenge remains the same: identity built on motion. Without stable cultural roots, many TCKs wrestle with “rootlessness” — a sense of belonging everywhere and nowhere at once. And for some, that rootlessness carries emotional consequences: difficulty forming long-term relationships, unresolved grief for lost connections, a persistent search for home. 

That, I realize now, is why I tell the details of my childhood — why I bring you into long playground walks in grey courtyards, whispered misunderstandings at school gates, bouquets abandoned on stone steps, and snow-covered Metro rides. Because those small moments, living in a city straddling Soviet legacy and global ambition, shaped a way of seeing the world rooted not in one culture, but in the tension between many. 

In 2025 — in a time of rising nationalism, migration crises, displaced populations, and global uncertainty — the insights of TCKs may be more than personal: they may point to how identity, home, and belonging evolve. They remind us that belonging doesn’t always come from roots; sometimes it comes from motion. That identity isn’t always stable; sometimes it’s a collage. And that home can be more than a place — it can be an experience, a memory, a question. 

For those who grew up between worlds, the stakes are not only personal but universal. Because as borders shift and cultures collide, perhaps the TCK way of being — layered, flexible, unsettled — becomes a map for how many of us live.
