“I saw it on Instagram,” a fifth-year Anthropology student at the New School said, when asked about how they learned their program was going to be indefinitely discontinued and eventually redesigned.

The climate around higher education is changing. With the Trump administration advocating for the reduction of racial diversity in universities, expanding barriers for international student enrollment, and cutting federal funding, the New School, with its over a century-long history of social justice and interdisciplinary practices, is not immune to these challenges. Currently, the university has a projected $48 million deficit, which the administration has cited as grounds for a massive restructuring plan. With all of the uncertainties coming to the community, one thing remains constant: anger around how leadership is communicating.

On November 26th, 2025, the New School’s student-run news organization, The New School Free Press, published a story revealing the university’s plans to cut or pause 25 programs; the previous day, staff and faculty received a list of impacted programs and planned austerity measures via email. Before this story was published, faculty and students alike were left guessing about what programs would be shut down, if any at all were to be ended. 

The most notable changes include reducing the New School into a two-college model, a stark difference from the 13 schools that made up the university in recent years. Another change is the discontinuing of programs, with the stated goal of redesigning them in the future. A few of the affected programs include History, Sociology, and Environmental Studies. There is also a pause in doctoral program admissions for the 2026-27 academic year, except for the clinical psychology department. Staff and faculty face cuts to compensation and retirement contributions, and roughly 40% of full-time faculty members received letters offering “voluntary separation” and “voluntary early retirement” packages in the fall 2025 semester, along with non-unionized staff members who received similar offers. Staff members of the Local 1205 union were offered packages in the spring 2026 semester. 

These voluntary measures cut 7% of faculty and staff, according to an email from President Joel Towers, a percent deduction that still fails to meet the administration’s 20% total reduction goal (mirroring the 20% decrease in enrollment since 2021).

After months of leadership warning that mass “involuntary separations,” or layoffs, were on the horizon, Provost Richard Kessler and Executive Vice President for Business & Operations and Chief Operating Officer ‌Francisco Pineda, finally announced to the community on March 13th, that these decisions would be finalized on June 1st.


Up until these recent, dramatic announcements, leadership had offered little information about the upcoming changes. Over the summer, faculty and staff were invited to be a part of “summer working groups,” in an attempt to come up with solutions to avoid the current measures. Besides what was discussed in these groups, [[Right: the information accessible to most of the staff, faculty, and student body was only circulated through leaked information and hearsay. Even when community members received communications, they were often incomplete or vague.]]

“We’ve been getting major announcements from leadership with little guidance or specificity on how and when structural changes, including layoffs and separations, will occur. This uncertainty creates mistrust,” said one Lang staff member.

The mistrust is felt on all levels, but especially by students. Students feel that decisions are set in stone and made without input. “The restructuring has been going on for so long and they’re only now giving us information,” the same 5th fifth-year anthropology student said. “It’s kind of too late to organize because everything is already happening.”

When asked if he trusted the administration, Ryan Desiderio, a first-year Lang student said, “Not at all.” He described instances of having to handle immense challenges on his own, like how to navigate issues with on-campus housing, and personal medical emergencies. “There are administrative or departmental emails at the school, [when] you email them, you get a response that is not signed by a person. So there's zero accountability,” he said. Desiderio emphasized that students struggle to get access to people who can help and important information, and when they do there isn’t a guarantee if they’ll get a response. 

When official information is shared, it’s through long, complex, and often confusing emails. Members of the New School community are dissatisfied with the level of compassion and transparency, something leadership said was a priority at a community forum. The responses vary from school to school. When asked about the general attitudes of Parsons students around the restructuring, Hannah Song, a Parsons senior said, “They don't know about it. They don't care.” That could be due to the unclear information or that different schools are impacted disproportionally. In a 2023 restructuring planning document, an idea was floated to merge the New School into a singular unit of Parsons. Many have argued there is a bias that higher administration members favor Parsons, while schools like the Eugene Lang College of Liberal Arts and New School for Social Research are usually at the forefront of devastating cuts and changes. 

“As per usual, communications from leadership have felt sudden, just before the holidays, and piecemeal,”another staff member shared. The staff member said that it appeared to be “by design to announce cuts and implement austerity measures at the end of term, as if to quell activity on campus.” 

Students and faculty are left with many unanswered questions, the main ones being: with an annual operating budget of $450M and 86% of that coming from tuition,where are tuition dollars going, and how did the school get into this large of a deficit in the first place? 

TNS has historically had a small endowment. For context, a school like Harvard has an endowment of $56.9 billion dollars, which is significantly higher than the New School’s current $500 million endowment. 

Administration cites dropping enrollment trends as a main factor.

According to the New School’s Financial Transparency Council, which is made up of representatives from the university’s colleges, administrative departments and offices, and the student, faculty, and staff senates, “even modest changes in enrollment have a significant impact,” given the school’s reliance on tuition money. “Enrollment has fallen by about 10% from historic levels,” they said, leading to three years of operating deficits.

“I worry that these proposed changes, many of which are claimed to be in reaction to low-enrollment, will only make enrollment worse,” said Madeline Trice, a Creative Writing MFA student. Some of these changes include last-minute class cancellations. Trice also alluded to the fact that if faculty are driven out by early retirement packages and layoffs, students won't have the same access to expert faculty they've grown accustomed to. 

“Canceling smaller classes and over burdening full time faculty will hurt students in the end,” Molly Kaufman, a part-time faculty member said. She also shared that, “The communication from the university has seemed top down.” She added that she was, however, grateful for the Part-Time Faculty union’s communications. “The PTF union has been making sure we are in the loop. My union has treated me like a valuable member of the community worthy of respect in this situation” — a sentiment many feel is missing from university leadership.

Many in the community believe the deficit is due to years of mismanagement, particularly with poor real estate investments and administrative salary bloat. Students, staff, and faculty are also confused by the gaps in what information is shared by the administration versus what they are experiencing, like the decision behind PhD admission pauses.President Towers has been publicly adamant that PhD programs lose the university revenue, and that it’s not sustainable to fund them, all while denying funding proposals worth $14 million from various members of the Board of Trustees. Faculty from NSSR have debunked the ideas that the PhD programs are contributing to the deficit, highlighting the difference between experience and information shared. 

Another example is the impact of higher administrative salaries. On the TNS Financial Transparency Council website, a series of charts break down the school’s finances, explaining that 47% of the budget is spent on faculty, staff, and student salaries. This, coupled with the focus on reducing the number of staff and faculty, particularly part-time faculty, could be considered a misled effort, when it’s reported that members of administration like the previous Provost Renée White made upwards of $618,605 in 2024. For context, the report also named a few of the top earning full-time faculty, who made around $300,000 and $400,000 in 2024. In 2023, if a part-time faculty member taught one course with 60 contact hours, they would make $6,700. Community members are left to wonder, what are the real implications of the highest salaries, particularly those of administrative salaries? Is reducing the number of staff and faculty actually going to make a difference if there are individuals making close to 1 million dollars? 

Students feel this institutional tension deeply. “The lack of real explanations as to what's going to happen and why makes me feel like leadership is actively working against the best interests of students, faculty, and staff,” Trice shared. “The only reason for lack of transparency in this situation…is that their actions and intentions are self-serving and to the detriment of the student body who ultimately fund this institution.”

Some students say the lack of transparency, accountability, and trust is negatively impacting their educational experience. On a practical level, current students and alumni are not sure if the validity of their degrees will withstand all of the merging and program pausing. A student in the joint BAMA program, whose Creative Publishing and Critical Journalism MA program will be merging with the Creative Writing MFA program asks, “I start CPCJ next year — what will that merging mean? What am I going to be studying? What will my masters be in?”


Students have been able to rely on the reporting from the New School Free Press for university updates. Andrea Robbin, the managing editor of the News and Opinion desks of the NSFP, shared that the students feel the coverage is helpful. When asked about her hopes for the future of the university, Robbin said, “I really hope that the New School is able to continue its roots. It's a progressive institution, and the core values of it are what makes the New School special. I just want it to remain a place for research, for progressive ideas, for exploration. It's really one of a kind, and I feel like right now that's in danger. So my hope would be for it to hold those values while growing.”

Many members of the TNS community are not only worried about program cuts and layoffs, but an ideological shift of the progressive nature of the school. With the humanities under attack nationwide, some are worried that the New School is following in this trend, which directly goes against the leftist origins of the university. This is particularly seen with the development of the Center for the American Experience, which is a “university-wide initiative [that] will explore the past, present, and future of the U.S. through an interdisciplinary lens.” A leaked document was also circulated that expressed some of the founding principles, such as, “At its core, the Center will uphold the principle of institutional neutrality, fostering an environment where pluralism, dialogue, and fearless debate can thrive.”

The New School, once known for its strong sense of social justice and dedicated to protesting injustice, has witnessed ideological shifts in years past already. From students being arrested during the Gaza Solidarity Encampment to admin taking more apolitical approaches to world events – so far not commenting on TNS being mentioned in the Epstein files, or any recent news events such as ICE raids in Minnesota that resulted in the killings of American citizens Renee Good and Alex Pretti, or the joint attacks on Iran by the United States and Israel – which differs from past presidents who made statements addressing current events, like President Dwight McBride’s statement on the murder of George Floyd. 

The unsettling feelings of mistrust and lack of respect from leadership throughout the New School community have only grown since the start of the 2020 COVID-19 Pandemic and the Part Time Faculty Strike in the fall of 2022. It’s clear that facing alarming economic challenges is not a new problem. “It’s haunting how similar to October 2020 this all feels,” a staff member said, when reflecting on the uncertainty and stress around the layoffs that are coming. 

The certainty of the New School’s future is unknown. Students, staff and faculty are scared – scared for the quality of their education, community, job security and the New School’s legacy.
